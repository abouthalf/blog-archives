Whitespace bias

Tags: design
Things
Monday, July 29th, 2013
Tweet

Whitespace or negative space in visual design and art is used to isolate elements from each other, to make type easier to read, and to convey emotional state. Whitespace is the empty space around things, called “white” because the presumably white paper is left unprinted. The space may not be truly empty, there may be a background color or texture, there may be distant trees or sky in a landscape, but it works the same way. Whitespace is distinct from and recedes from the subject.

This historic Coca-Cola advertisement uses lots of space around the product to focus your attention on the product and the message that Coke is, in fact, the real thing (like baseball). This isolates the product and the message from the rest of an otherwise busy magazine.

This book cover crowds the page with larger than life text, too big for the page, too big for the photo. Here the text, the writing, the work, is larger than the man who wrote the text.

The MacBook Air web page uses lots of space around the type and the product to emphasize the lightness and size if the product.

The space around the UI elements in “Byword” for iPad makes for easier reading, naturally, but it also makes the elements easier to physically tap. It increases the overall size of the touch-target.

Premium empty space

Cheaply printed materials like phone books and newspapers (ask your parents) have as little whitespace as they can possibly get away with. For these items to be useful and profitable, they have to be widely circulated and therefore cheap to print. They’re made from the cheapest paper that will do the job, and that paper is covered in ink. More information on fewer pages is less costly to print and less costly to ship and less costly to deliver.

Expensive printed items, like your favorite coffee table art book, are made from higher quality paper which is nice to touch and to look at. These are much more expensive. Far fewer are printed. They are prized possessions; they are status symbols. The makers of these items can afford to make each page into a brightly lit photo gallery with ample whitespace to isolate their subjects and imbue them with a sense of serious grandeur.

We associate ample whitespace with quality. We associate puny whitespace with junk. These associations have become biases. Designers will often prefer more whitespace over less, even in digital media like web sites and app icons where the extra empty space costs nothing.[1]

Applied bias

Apple recently announced iOS 7 for iPhones and iPads. The new operating system features a pretty bold redesign of the user interface. The home-screen icons for Apple’s default suite of applications have all new icons which have been designed around a new grid system based upon concentric rings centered in a rounded square. This video from Apple’s website shows how the grid system works, animating a progression of system icons through a grid-based construction.

This post by Neven Mrgan claims that this new grid is all wrong (or is at least being used wrong). His argument is centered around whitespace, or more specifically, that the grid doesn’t provide enough whitespace. He makes his case by showing us an Apple-created grid-based icon, and then showing us his tweaked version with more “air” around the icon. He argues that the outer ring of the icon grid is too big, and therefore bad. His version of the icon is “optically adjusted” to fit “better”.

He provides no concrete reasons why the adjusted version is better. No discussion of readability, nor how an icon with more whitespace might work better for some uses. He states, referring to his tweaked icon:

To our eyes—and we get paid to have good ones, we’re told—this is more correct.

This is an argument from taste[2] and an argument from authority. A perfect example of applied whitespace bias.

It’s a trap

Like with this previous heading, it’s easy to fall into a rut with creative work. A designer might fall back to premium whitespace instead of working the composition, while a developer might fall back to writing a web app safely in PHP instead of trying something new.

I’m a fan of the new iOS icons. I think the colors are fresh and current; I think the grid is a smart guideline; and I prefer the larger, more readable pictographs. The lack of whitespace is not a problem because (almost) all of these icons will be used on very high resolution screens which won’t suffer from blurry, pixel-y edges and lines.

What I like most, though, is that the boldness of the design has shaken up opinions and exposed the conservative rut so many of us get caught in.

  1. Too much or too little white space can cost in terms of failing or succeeding as a design, but the pixels are free.  ↩

  2. There are other nits I could pick. He doesn’t consider what a screen full of his optically adjusted icons will look like. He fails to acknowledge that the larger the subject (the picture part of the icon), the easier it will be to read on a small screen — a small screen packed with icons laid over a busy photo of your kids. He’s not considering the use case of an app icon on a phone’s home screen. Just his tastes in whitespace.  ↩
