I’ve been thoroughly nerd-ing out over the “Tron” franchise recently. I re-watched both the original 80s movie and the sequel. I’ve been enjoying Tron: Uprising on iTunes. I read the graphic novel Tron: Betrayal. I am well-Tronned at this point.

The visuals of the original 80s “Tron” hold up really well even if the plot doesn’t. The original movie treated computers as magical and programmers as wizards. The Master Control Program (the film’s main villain) communicated with its human counterparts in plain English. I think even Siri would find that a little hard to accept.

“Tron: Legacy” improves upon the visual effects of “Tron” in every conceivable way. While it looks absolutely great, it oddly lacks the “wow” factor of the original movie. We live in a post-Jurassic-Park world and visual effects alone are not enough to stun us. I already believe a man can fly.

These movies are not known for their depth, but, they touch on big ideas and tease at deeper concepts. This is what makes them compelling (that and a Daft Punk sound track).

Both films dabble in spirituality and philosophy. In the original “Tron” the programs which inhabit the digital world revere their human users in a religious fashion. The red-glowing villains of the film discourage any belief in human “users”, like state-enforced atheism. Perhaps a thinly veiled reference to communism under the Soviet Union. The programs get to meet a user when the protagonist, Kevin Flynn, is laser-zapped into the digital world. The programs literally get to meet their maker, or at least meet a maker. Imagine meeting god only to discover he lives above the arcade he works in to avoid paying rent.

“Tron: Legacy” takes a more literary route to spirituality. Kevin Flynn has created an entire system himself and has populated it with programs who “live” to fulfill their purpose. Here Kevin Flynn is the creator. The villain in “Tron: Legacy” is Clu, a programmatic copy of Kevin Flynn who wages a war to seize control of this digital Eden. Kevin Flynn’s son, Sam, then arrives to save everyone. “Tron: Legacy” is “Paradise Lost” in video-game form.

On a less biblical note, the inhabitants of the digital worlds of “Tron” get to enjoy a sort of hands-on Platonism. Their world is an expression of “code” – both users and programs can see and understand the fundamental nature of their world, alter the code, and see those changes expressed in the world around them. “Tron: Uprising“ uses this trope to great effect with the main character Beck, a digital mechanic. Beck can unfold any solid looking object into a set of glowing shapes and volumes, tinker with their arrangement, then make them whole again. It’s like being able to reach into something, extract the idea that defines it, play with it, and then put it back to see what happens.

“Tron” is a straight-forward fantasy-adventure film. It abuses computer terminology and plays fast and loose with realism. “Tron: Legacy” avoids concrete computer terms, at least most of the time, and tries to ground the film in plausible scientific concepts with throw-away dialog referencing quantum teleportation in an attempt to lend credibility to the fantasy. Science advisors were brought in to help.

Once you start grounding things in real science, you start to run into the Star Trek problem. Famously, when the original “Star Trek” series was being produced in the 60s, they didn’t have the budget to create the special effect of a starship landing on a new planet every week. So they came up with the idea of the transporter. The phrase “beam me up, Scotty” was born. As the “Star Trek” franchise matured, they worked harder to ground the writing with science. Things like the warp drive are based in real, if impractical, physics. But the transporter, being a convenience for writers and producers, wasn’t thought out in advance. The writers treated it inconsistently, and technical consultants had to apply phlebotinum to explain it away. In short, the Star Trek problem is the attempt to make a bit of outlandish fantasy realistic, therefore making it completely unbelievable.

In “Tron: Legacy” we learn that Kevin Flynn used to zap himself in and out of his computer all the time. If his molecules were separated (and stored?), their quantum states imported into a computer, and then everything reassembled later, was that really Kevin Flynn each time? Or did Kevin Flynn die a thousand deaths, one for every time he went into his office? Was the man that emerged from the laser beam the same man or not? We’re supposed to assume that, yes, it’s the same man. But who is Clu, if not a digital copy of the same data? Is he not Kevin Flynn too? And if so, why doesn’t Clu get older? All of this is a corollary to the Star Trek Problem which I like to call “the existential crisis of teleportation”. It would make a good horror film.
