Introverts in public

Things
Saturday, December 14th, 2013
Tweet

Matt Haughey, of Metafilter, writes “An Introvert’s guide to better presentations”.

Matt shares his advice for overcoming the fear of standing in front of a room full of people, hot to prepare for your presentation, and what your slides should look like (hint: lay off the bullet points, Poindexter).

It’s a great little article with good advice. It’s advice that your boss probably needs more than you…but it’s good advice nonetheless. His descriptions of butterflies and rooms full of eyes looking your way reminds me of the last time I was in front of a room full of people.

Back in September my company sent me to Osaka, Japan to help with the installation of a large HTML5 touch screen application. My job was to be the person on-site to help with any bugs or problems with the software set-up and to help explain the system to my colleagues in Japan.

The application was installed on large, 40” touch screens at a newly renovated retail store. While the renovation was completed, the store employees were undergoing training for the store’s grand re-opening. One night, after that day’s training was completed, my team was invited to join everyone at a big celebratory dinner. Our hosts introduced around to everyone. We enjoyed classic Osaka seafood pancakes. I impressed my hosts by knowing how to flip my chopsticks around to take food from a communal plate. When you’re sharing from a large platter of food, you should flip your chopsticks around and take food with the blunt ends – the ends that haven’t been in your mouth. There were speeches, prizes, lots of crisp Japanese beer, and endless toasts. Towards the end of the evening I was asked if I might be willing to drop by the training session the next day and speak about how the application works and how it should be used. I was unsure. The beer said “yes”.

The next morning I spent a couple of hours creating a custom build of the application that I could run, scaled down, on my laptop — as long as I had a good internet connection (we would, right?). I made a little outline. I was feeling confident. My teammate, just in case, called back to Portland to ask if we could get a video of store employees using our local installation.

We arrived at the hotel conference center where training was being held. I began to get set up. I asked about internet access. There was no internet access. Without internet access, I had no presentation. But we still had the video from Portland! I hadn’t watched it. What to do?

When in doubt try honesty. I plugged up my laptop to the projector. I was introduced. I brought up the video. I explained haltingly, so my interpreter could keep up, that unfortunately I would not be able to give the presentation that I had planned. But let’s see what our friends in Portland made…

I played through the video; it was funny! What a relief. With that out of the way I realized I could use the video as a presentation, skimming ahead to show different parts of the application. I started with a summary. I explained how we’d go to the video for an example of each step in the application. I talked a little, played some video, paused, pointed out some things on screen, and asked for questions.

It actually worked out pretty well. At the end I discovered that my teammate, who had the foresight to request that video in the first place, was standing as far back in the corner as possible. I discovered later that my colleagues had video-recorded me speaking. This video made it back home to my colleagues. It made me look like a college professor, like I knew what I was doing.

Next time I have a presentation of working software, I’ll take pictures before and maybe make a video. Just in case.
