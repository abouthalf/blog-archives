In defense of iPad photographers

Tags: iPad, photos
Things
Tuesday, November 20th, 2012
Tweet

It’s a cliche now to poke fun of people taking photos with an iPad, and it’s not hard to see why. A full-sized iPad held at arm’s length is awkward and ungainly. The big 10” tablet blocks your view if you hold it too close (maybe to block glare from the sun). Your choices are to balance an awkward slab of glass at a distance or engage in a furious bout of chimping to see around your tablet. One should admit: if all you have is your iPad, and an opportunity for a photo comes along, you should use your iPad. The best camera is the one you have with you. No matter how goofy it looks.[1]

Not long ago I watched some of my in-laws chase their new granddaughter around with an iPad, taking pictures and video. Lots of pictures and video. I saw none of the usual fumbling and resetting that usually accompanies family photos. No taking “just one more to be safe” with exactly the same settings in the same lighting and the same pose. Just click, click, click: Photos.

The new large format

If you’ve ever seen a photographer with an old-school large format film camera, you can easily imagine an iPad in its place. The same sort of wonky, physical, handling applies. You have to negotiate a large viewfinder to compose your shot.

The main benefit of a large format film camera, of course, was/is the tremendously higher quality (compared to 35mm film, for example). These cameras used big 4” × 6” or or 8” × 10” film to capture a great amount of light and detail. If you ever have the opportunity to see an Ansel Adams photograph in person, get real close. You can count pine-needles on the trees.

The second benefit of these cameras, though, is the big view-finder which allows the photographer to compose a shot with two eyes (instead of one eye peering through a tiny hole) at arm’s length or so – a distance closer to the real-life viewing distance of a printed photograph. The very same is true for taking a photo with an iPad.

Today’s iPad 4 features a camera which will take 5 mega-pixel photos. That’s not great by the standards of high-end camera gear, but it’s pretty good. And certainly good enough for most needs. These little cameras are only going to get better. iPads and other tables are only going to get faster (and cheaper). Combine the better cameras with better hardware, and I won’t be surprised to see photo studios with iPads clamped to a tripod. Click it and flip it around. How’s this? Another? Click again.

No-hour photos

iPads, iPhones, and the like are, of course, small computers optimized for carrying around with you. These little computers can run photo-editing software. Facebook recently paid billions for some of that software (oh and I guess a social network too).

Conventional digital photography workflows followed these 4 steps:

  1. Take photos
  2. Transfer to computer
  3. Process photos in software
  4. Print, store, or burn to a disk

Shooting photos on an iPad or an iPhone removes steps 2 and 4 – as you can use photo editing software right on the iPad, and the iPad itself is probably the most frequently used display or output device for those photos.

Magic picture frame

iPads combine photo taking, editing, storing, and displaying into one object which looks like a picture frame. Anyone who is unafraid to look a little silly can hold up this picture frame, view the world through it, and capture what they see. The image you see through the iPad becomes the image you keep.

There is no abstraction. I see this. I want to keep this. I kept this. There’s no concept of SD cards, USB cables, or transferring photos from this device to that computer. Just pictures. Oh. And video. Because you can do all of this with video too, on the same device, with the same immediacy.

Looking a little silly is a small price to pay.

  1. There’s more than a little classism in those jokey internet GIFs. If someone had only $500 to spend on a phone, a camera, or a tablet (which does some of all three), they can’t be faulted for getting the most out of the one device they have. It’s easy for well-paid nerds to forget that not everyone can afford a laptop, digital SLR, smartphone, and a tablet computer.  ↩

Ita for your lists

Tags: iphone
Reviews
Saturday, October 20th, 2012
Tweet

I’ve been using Ita as my go-to to-do list for months now. I am not a fan of big GTD applications like OmniFocus or “full featured” to-do list apps like Things. These apps are both great (so I hear) but are wrong for the way I have to function in the world.

If I find myself self-employed or running a small business at some point, I can imagine running things with a dedicated super-app like OmniFocus. But today I have a day-job and my job has a ticketing system and that ticketing system integrates with a wiki, version control, code review tools, and testing systems.

Things is good looking software. I’ve tried it. It works well. But, it is locked into the iOS ecosystem and I want more flexibility an it provides.

I use Evernote to solve this problem. I can use Evernote as an everything-bucket to store research and notes, I can use it to create a simple to-do list, and if I have a bigger project I can create a folder and process everything GTD style or use it as a primitive tool for Agile style development.

More importantly though, Evernote is available on multiple platforms and the web. It’s also free for most users.

So if Evernote is so great why don’t I marry it? And why do I use Ita? Not every day is a work day.

For the weekend warrior

My wife is a harried medical student. I support her by taking care of most of the cleaning, laundry, shopping, and all of the cooking. Fortunately for both of us I would rather be in the kitchen than be most places.

In order to get everything done over the course of a short weekend, I use lists. Short lists. When the lists are done I throw them away. I don’t need to remember that I did the laundry on Saturday. I do the laundry most every Saturday. If the lay dry was special that day, I would have taken a picture.

Right next to the to-do list is the grocery list, which I add to over the course of the week.

Adding to a list is where Ita shines. Type, hit next, type, hit next. Fast and easy. To enter “add” mode you only tap “add” once, now you can only add to your list. Hit “done” when done.

Ita works well with the iOS dictation feature. As I run around the apartment preparing to do the shopping, I walk from one room to the next closet telling my phone things like “detergent” or “paper towels”. I am making my shopping list one-handed.

Processing your list (e.g. marking things off) is even easier. Tap the list item. It greys out and moves to the bottom. Done.. Easy.

Ita allows sharing of a list via copy, email, text message, or printing. Why you would print a list is beyond me, but hey. It’s an option. The sharing feature allows me to zip a grocery list to over to my wife via text message on the off chance that she can get to the store after rounds. Sharing isn’t a feature I need often, but I’m glad it’s there if I need it.

Ita is handsome to look at, super easy to use, costs only 99¢ in the App Store, and breaks the tyranny of wasteful, stupid little spiral note pads.
