I was catching up on back issues of The Magazine a while back and enjoyed this article a lot:

The Wet Shave by Lex Friedman.

I nodded in agreement throughout the piece. When I discovered wet shaving about 3 years ago, I told everyone I could find with a beard. It’s just so much better and so much cheaper.

Sure, my safety razor doesn’t have 5 blades. But it does have a box of 100 blades waiting in the medicine cabinet.

I bought my Parker on Amazon for about $30. It came with a box of 5 blades. The cost of a solid brass razor and 5 weeks worth of shaving costs as much a box of 10 lousy cartridge blades. Add a box of 100 blades for about $15 and you’re practically shaving for free for the next three years.

Shaving is a solved problem: Sharp, cheap blades and a well made tool that lasts a lifetime. The vibrating, multi-blade, lubra-smooth strip manufacturers of the world are solving a different problem — separating fools from their money.

Over the Christmas holiday I found a little bit of quiet time to get a project off the ground.

Years ago, when my grandparents passed away, my family uncovered (rediscovered?) a recipe book, handwritten by my great grandfather on my mother’s side. The book itself was an old ledger, lined paper with vertical columns for figures. Its green cover was tattered and worn, the pages were yellowed and finger-smudged from use. At the time, my mother borrowed it to try to make a copy before it went back home to Tennessee. I photographed and scanned the entire book; I printed and bound a few copies for sharing.

The book resonated with me powerfully. Over the years I’d page through the scans of the book and think about finding some way to share it. I ultimately decided upon a website.

  • ArchiesRecipes.com
  • Archie’s Recipes on Twitter (site updates)

I began by transcribing a few pages. The images of the pages are lovely, but if you can’t read (or search) the copy, they don’t do much good. After I had a few pages worth of recipes transcribed, I played around with some design ideas, settling on a spare design which emphasized the scanned pages and transcriptions and provided easy navigation between pages. I use media queries to format a side-by-side layout for larger monitors, and an image inset for smaller monitors, portrait tablets and legacy browsers. On tiny screens or phones the layout reduces to a single column.

The innards

At first I planned to “simply” use an existing publishing platform like WordPress to run the site. That never set right with me. WordPress suffers from feature bloat, especially for small, mostly static sites. I didn’t need plugins or remote editing. I didn’t want comments on the site. But still I know WordPress fairly well, and I had the guts of a simple single page theme ready and waiting. It seemed like a good, quick approach.

Then, after my recent WordPress security problems, I decided “no, maybe not WordPress. Definitely not.”

For a brief moment I thought about making an entirely static site, perhaps using CodeKit’s new HTML compilation syntax to ease production. I noodled with that for a bit and realized that I’d have no separation between my written content and the published output. I also realized that if I wanted to add any sort of feature in the future, I’d have to have some kind of CMS or publishing tool anyway.

I prefer to have my written content, whether its for this blog, notes at work, or whatever, in plain text. Plain text can go anywhere, on any system, and can be copied and pasted into anything. I shun word processors unless I need something precisely formatted (like an envelope). I write most everything, these days, in Byword using Markdown syntax. Of course, since I’m writing in plain text, the editor doesn’t matter. But I like Byword. It’s low-impact, works on my iPad, and exports tidy, clean XHTML 5.

The least amount of CMS possible.

Exporting from Byword to XHTML gives me a clean and tidy content repository of naked, simple, portable content. All I really needed to do was link it up and wrap some formatting around it.

Using the micro-framework Silex, and PHP’s native XML handling tools, I built a very lightweight publishing framework. All content lives in an ‘html’ directory. I use the Twig template engine to provide layouts. Pages are named simply ‘index.html’ or ‘about.html’ or whatever. Blog posts are named with a timestamp and a URL slug, like ‘2013–01–01T12–00–00 new-post.html’. Requests to the CMS simply find a matching file, extract the content and metadata, and push that data into a page template. The time-stamped blog posts make it trivial to have a path like /blog/2012 present a filtered list of posts authored in that year (e.g. blog post files whose name starts with “2012”). The RSS feed works in a similar fashion, by taking the most recent blog posts and injecting them into RSS XML output.

This semi-static publishing framework avoids the overhead of a database, of maintaining and updating blogging software, while making it possible to add other features in the future. I can easily update the layout of the site by pushing up new layout templates. Any new content in the repository becomes easily available.

Publishing

I could “publish” new pages to the site by simply pushing new files up via SFTP – or by even just writing new content directly on the server. That would be workable, if inelegant. Instead I set up a GIT repository on my server, and used a ‘post hook’ to publish updates to the web site.

This is nice and tidy. Now I can just work on the site, updating content, the CMS, the layout and design, and just commit locally and push. Magically the site is updated.

Refreshing

I like WordPress – it’s a powerful tool (perhaps too powerful for its original, intended purpose) and it is available everywhere. But in this case it offered more than I needed and more work than I wanted. I stuck with PHP out of laziness and convenience. I didn’t want to add a learning curve to my personal deadline [1].

This was a very refreshing way to build a site. I built just what I needed in only a couple of days. In the end I got something small, light, and easy to maintain. Something that shouldn’t have any performance problems if it gets popular. If it does, it will be trivial to add caching. Because there’s a thin application wrapper around the content, I can easily add more features. Because the content is just plain HTML (and plain text before that) I can do anything I want with my content.

  1. Sensio Labs is probably making the best PHP frameworks available today. They take advantage of the best, most modern features of the language and shun legacy junk. If you need to use PHP, you could do a lot worse.  ↩
