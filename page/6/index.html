What is Medium anyway?

Tags: medium
Things
Thursday, December 5th, 2013
Tweet

I just saw this post at Daring Fireball about some new features available at Medium.

Mr. Gruber writes:

I still don’t get what Medium is.

I believe Medium is slightly insidious. Medium is a land-grab. I think it’s an attempt to lock up the “serious” online writing space. It entices authors with a handsome, low-commitment tool for writing which is easier than maintaining a blog or a hosting a website full of articles. It’s more “serious” and less “fun” than Tumblr. WordPress (the hosted and self-hosted varieties) hovers in between a full content management system and a blogging engine. Unless you have a skilled designer/developer in your pocket, WordPress sites are usually ugly and lack good community or promotional features. Medium looks great[1] (with no work on the part of the author), it looks serious, and does a good job of promoting content through collections, recommendations, Twitter feeds, RSS feeds, and social media features.

Medium’s goal (I believe) is to become the default location on the web for people to find long-form articles, much like Twitter is the default chat room for the internet.

Why is this insidious? Medium presents itself like a social media site focused on long-form writing. Most “content” on social media web sites is worthless. Facebook posts are drivel; Tweets are mostly noise. But Medium is attracting writers who can produce things of quality and lasting value. Anything you contribute to a social media site (links, comments, pluses, likes, up-votes) only serves to make that social media site more valuable to its audience. The more friends and family you have on Facebook the more valuable Facebook is. The more funny cat pictures on Reddit, the more Reddit-y it is. When an author writes something for Medium, that author is giving away their work and their audience for almost nothing[2] while making Medium a better, richer site. This means Medium can slowly become the news, opinion, and editorial source for the web, without paying journalists or writers.

  1. Medium’s aesthetic lends a lot of credibility to the writing hosted there. Medium has a lot of good content, but a lot of it is pretty inane. However, it doesn’t look inane because Medium presents writing so well.  ↩

  2. Sure, you might get some followers and some notoriety on Medium, but you can’t host ads on Medium. There’s not a clear way for a good writer to make a living using Medium.  ↩

After installing a recent update to iOS 7 (iOS 7.0.4), my iPad mini suddenly played distorted sound from the built-in speakers. How am I supposed to listen to Lady Gaga’s ArtPop while I work now?

If you experience this you can try to fix this with a settings reset. Go to Settings -> General -> Reset -> Reset All Settings. This will wipe out some network settings, sound settings, and a few other easily fixed things, but leave your iPad unharmed with data and media intact. This returned my iPad’s internal speakers to their slightly tinny normal state.

I’ve always thought of software testing like tooth brushing. Important, necessary, but slightly tedious. I never took to true test-first development because it felt burdensome.

On my current project we inherited a big suite of tests implemented in JsTestDriver. All of my impressions of testing remained in tact. JsTestDriver is a good tool, I don’t mean to disparage it, but it inherits a “Java” mentality. One big test for each big object. Clunky. Slow.

Over the weekend I experimented with the Karma test runner, using Mocha as my test framework and Chai as my assertion library. Suddenly the air was sweeter.

Karma runs in NodeJs; it starts up in a flash. Mocha defaults to a behavior-driven model. I find this to be more intuitive and to encourage writing smaller, more granular tests. But that’s not the important thing.

For the first time it was practical to have my test runner automatically run tests as I code. This is a new world – code executing while I type it. Now test-first development programming makes sense. You write an implementation of the code as you write the code, and then with the help of a smart tool it executes and runs all the while.

So smart.

Hollywood Video

Tags: portland
Things
Sunday, November 10th, 2013
Tweet

A Hollywood Video used to be here.

On the front wall, above the cash registers, was a large hand-painted mural depicting a scene from Star Trek III: The Search for Spock, complete with blue neon making the U.S.S. Enterprise look extra science-y.

When my wife and I began dating back in 2004, we would stop by the adjoining grocery store for a bottle of cheap wine, and then peruse the aisles looking for something to watch together. Renting a movie was just an excuse to be together. The movie didn’t matter. Not much anyway. We would cruise the aisles looking for something that was good, but not too good, something we both hadn’t seen a million times, something we could easily ignore if we wanted to.

Sometimes the choice was easy. Some big new movie was finally out on video and picking it up was a no-brainer. Other times circling the store felt like circling a drain. How could there be nothing here? I don’t care. I just want to go home. With her.

Eventually I moved into a big-boy apartment and the video store was no longer on the beaten path. So-called ‘on demand’ cable filled in the gaps and then iTunes and Netflix.

Hollywood Video closed and sat empty for years. The dismal street-level top floor held the decaying collection of VHS tapes. While the store sat empty this became the home for strange seasonal window displays. Were they poorly conceived advertising or poorly conceived community outreach? Once I looked into a dusty window to see that the neon had been removed from the mural.

Last week, on my way to catch the train for work, I noticed that the building had gone missing. The sudden destruction of the Hollywood Video laid on me with the full weight of 11 years of forgotten history. Wow. That’s gone now. I’m still here, and that place where I used to go is gone. That dumb, ugly, concrete box of a video store became a mile-marker for my life. Now that it’s gone I wonder what I missed as I drove by.
